The problem: In my Data Visualization and Infographic class at the Rhode Island School of Design, my professor asked us to create an infographic that compiled at least twenty different infographics for one country.

The solution: I created this accordion book of Japan. The book is cut so that the left folio is two inches shorter than the right folio. The top two inches of the right folio are cut so that when folded up, the layers create an image.

One of my favorite parts about this product is the layered paper effect in each right folio. For the final copy, I actually printed out the pages and used an exacto-knife to cut out the layers. I wanted each page to add a layer of Japanese culture to the image when the book was completely folded up. After researching Japanese history, I ultimately decided on including a tsunami wave, Buddhist temples, bonsai trees, mount fuji, and the Japanese flag as my layers. While these do not fully encapsulate Japanese culture, they are significant in the country’s history and form a nice design when completely folded up.

I chose a book design for this product initially because I thought it would create a neat effect with a timeline that continues through each page, and also because it was a good challenge for layout. I chose an ornamental sans-serif for the headers and geometric sans-serif for the copy as an attempt of combining the old and the new, as symbolic of Japan’s history. I knew I wanted to use red as it is the color of the Japanese flag. I ultimately decided on teal as a secondary color because I felt that it added to the product without distracting from the red. I also felt teal resembled nature and the ocean which are pillars of Japan.
